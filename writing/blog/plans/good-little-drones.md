# "Good Little Drones"

## On the Death of Hacker Counterculture and Who Killed It

---

## Title & Framing

**Title**: "Good Little Drones"
**Subtitle**: _On how Silicon Valley bought the counterculture and nobody noticed until it was too late_

**Backup titles** (in order of preference):

1. _"Mistrust Authority (Terms and Conditions Apply)"_
2. _"We Grew Up Fighting This"_
3. _"Subculture Is Not Solidarity"_

---

## Core Thesis

We grew up as hackers believing that information should be free, that authority was illegitimate, and that the suits were the enemy. Then the suits started signing our paycheques — and it turns out that was enough. The hacker counterculture didn't get defeated. It got acquired.

The betrayal is not that tech workers became rich. It's that they were convinced they were _already_ insiders — that they were _"we"_ — and so the billionaire class's enemies became their enemies, all while the actual power, the actual wealth, and the actual decisions remained exactly where they always were.

But there's a harder truth underneath: we mistook subcultural identity for political commitment. A soldering iron is not a political education. Anti-authoritarian _aesthetics_ can bend toward mutual aid and solidarity, or toward libertarianism, hierarchy, and elite capture. When we relied on vibes instead of structures, we made ourselves easy to buy.

---

## Argument Structure (The Through-Line)

```
PREMISE 1: Hacker culture was genuinely counterculture — documented, principled, anti-authoritarian
    ↓
PREMISE 2: But Silicon Valley was never "outside the system" — it was entangled
           with state and military power from the start
    ↓
PREMISE 3: The Hacker Ethic was co-optable because it spoke in values, not
           material interests — subculture is not politics
    ↓
PREMISE 4: Elites captured that language and manufactured a false "we" — making
           workers feel like stakeholders through compensation architecture
    ↓
PREMISE 5: When workers did resist, they were punished — not just by management,
           but by their own peers. The crabs pulled each other back in.
    ↓
PREMISE 6: Corporate ethics language proved reversible the moment it conflicted
           with strategic interests
    ↓
CONCLUSION: The answer is not "find better nerds" — it is "build better
            institutions." Subcultural identity will not save us. Durable
            collective structures might.
```

## Section Breakdown

---

### I. OPENING: The Noisebridge Problem (~400 words)

**Purpose**: Establish emotional truth and credibility. Pose the essay's central question through lived experience.

**Hook**: San Francisco's Noisebridge — the hackerspace that everyone said was the real thing. Counterculture. Anti-authoritarian. Doing it different.

**The tell**: It was _weird_ in a way that felt off. Counterculture, yes — but _prescribed_ counterculture. Rules about how to be transgressive. Ideological orthodoxy dressed as radical openness. The transgression had been audited and approved.

**The contrast**: London's hackerspaces. Edinburgh's. Manchester's. The same toolkit — soldering irons, 3D printers, someone's ancient CNC mill — but the _politics_ was different. Anarchosocialist by default, not by charter. Queer disasters grumbling about the state, not performing a brand identity of "disruption."

**The pivot**: Why did the SF hacker scene feel so different? What had the proximity to money and power actually done to it? And — the harder question — did we mistake a cultural style for a political guarantee?

**Thesis by paragraph three or four**: state it cleanly. The essay asks why people from supposedly anti-authoritarian tech cultures end up defending structures they once hated, and argues that the answer lies in incentives, institutions, and a category error about what subculture can do.

**Tone**: First person, analytical. Angry with systems and incentives, not just individual hypocrisy.

---

### II. WHERE WE CAME FROM: The Hacker Ethic Was Real (~500 words)

**Purpose**: Establish the heritage honestly — not romantically, but accurately. Concede the grain of truth: the early hacker counterculture had genuine teeth. Then set up its vulnerability.

**Key evidence**:

- **The Hacker Ethic** (Steven Levy, _Hackers_, 1984): "Mistrust authority — promote decentralisation." Information sharing as moral good. Judged by your code, not your credentials. Not just aesthetics — principled rejection of institutional hierarchy.
- **The Jargon File**: _"Hackers are naturally anti-authoritarian. Anyone who can give you orders can stop you from solving whatever problem you're being fascinated by."_
- **The Homebrew Computer Club** (Menlo Park, 1975): Co-founded by Fred Moore, a draft resister. The Altair circuit board reverse-engineered and handed out at meetings. Before VC, before IPOs — just people who believed information should travel freely.
- **The Cypherpunks** (1992 onwards): Eric Hughes's _A Cypherpunk's Manifesto_ (1993): "Privacy is necessary for an open society in the electronic age." PGP distributed as civil disobedience. The NSA listed as the adversary. These people were _fighting_ something.
- **The underground press** — _Phrack_, _2600_ — circulating technical knowledge as deliberate information liberation.
- **The Whole Earth Catalog** and Stewart Brand's 1995 _Time_ essay: _"We Owe It All to the Hippies."_ Fred Turner's _From Counterculture to Cyberculture_ traces how Brand synthesised Bay Area counterculture with personal computing as liberation technology.

**The through-line**: All of these operated on the same premise — that the powerful hoarded information and capability, and that sharing it freely was a radical act. The hacker was someone who found a way in that the gatekeepers didn't intend.

**The point**: This culture was _real_. It wasn't invented by a marketing team. It had genuine anti-authoritarian content. That matters — because co-opting something real is a different operation than just inventing a brand.

**The pivot (setting up Section III)**: But the Hacker Ethic's genius — and its fatal flaw — was that it described values without describing material interests. And Silicon Valley was never the outsider insurgency its mythology claimed.

---

### III. THE CATEGORY ERROR: Subculture Is Not Politics (~500 words)

**Purpose**: Disarm the strongest objection early ("but hackers are weird outsiders!") and establish the historical entanglement that makes the "betrayal" narrative incomplete.

**The category error**:

- Technical curiosity, DIY practice, and distrust of authority do not automatically produce left politics. Those traits can bend toward mutual aid and solidarity, or toward libertarianism, hierarchy, and elite capture.
- The mistake is treating style and aesthetic as a substitute for political economy. "Information wants to be free" says nothing about who owns the servers. "Meritocracy" says nothing about who defines merit. "Disruption" says nothing about who benefits from what gets disrupted.
- The Hacker Ethic spoke in _values_, not _material interests_. That made it co-optable.

**The older contradiction — Silicon Valley was never outside the system**:

- DARPA's ARPANET: the roots of the modern internet lie in 1960s defence research. First computer-to-computer signal (UCLA to SRI) on 29 October 1969.
- Fairchild Semiconductor's early orders were tied to IBM Federal Systems Division (B-70 onboard computer); Autonetics selected a Fairchild device for the Minuteman ballistic missile guidance system.
- This isn't "the internet is military so everything is evil." It's "the industry has always been a hybrid of public money, military demand, academic research, and private extraction — so stop pretending it was ever politically innocent."

**The framing**: The "Californian ideology" — counterculture rhetoric fused with market libertarianism and technological determinism. Anti-state language alongside dependence on state infrastructure, funding, and demand. The contradiction is older than today's AI boom.

**Key line**: "A soldering iron is not a political education."

---

### IV. THE ACQUISITION: How the Language Was Stolen (~600 words)

**Purpose**: Show the specific mechanisms by which hacker values were linguistically captured and inverted.

**Step 1: Translate the vocabulary into corporate legitimacy**

The founders who built Silicon Valley used hacker language explicitly. The garage mythology. "Don't be evil." "Move fast and break things." The norms of the Homebrew Club — share freely, judge by output, hierarchy is bullshit — became startup culture, minus the sharing and minus the anti-hierarchy.

The words were kept. The politics were stripped.

**Step 2: Manufacture the in-group through compensation architecture**

The billionaire class doesn't need every tech worker to be a true believer. They just need tech workers to feel like they're _on the same side_.

The tools:

- **Equity and stock options**: You don't just work for the company; you _own_ part of it. RSUs generally vest over four years contingent on continued employment. Alphabet alone reserved 534 million shares for future issuance under its 2021 stock plan; total stock-based compensation expense was $22.1B (2023), $22.8B (2024), $27.1B (2025). When the government proposes to tax unrealised gains, that's an attack on _you_ — even if you're holding 0.0001% and the founder is holding 40%.
- **The wage differential**: BLS data (May 2024) — median annual wage for software developers: $133,080. All occupations: $49,500. Silicon Valley averages push to $164,737. These aren't just wages — they're golden handcuffs. Vesting schedules, retention bonuses, and RSU cliffs create a financial architecture specifically designed to make leaving — or rocking the boat — economically devastating.
- **Parasocial founder relationships**: Elon Musk replies to your tweet. Marc Andreessen wrote that essay you agreed with. Peter Thiel is transgressive and smart and so are you. The intimacy is manufactured but the feeling is real.
- **Mission framing**: You're not shipping features — you're _changing the world_. The teleological framing of the Hacker Ethic ("technology improves humanity") gets weaponised to mean "therefore, maximising our growth serves humanity." Carolyn Chen's _Work Pray Code_ argues Silicon Valley has replaced religion with work: tech professionals look to work for meaning, belonging, purpose, and transcendence — making dissent feel like self-destruction.
- **Shared enemy construction**: The enemies of the billionaire class (regulators, unions, "legacy media," governments that want to tax them) are reframed as enemies of _innovation_, enemies of _you_, enemies of _the future_.

**Step 3: Make dissent feel like betrayal of the tribe**

Once you feel like a stakeholder, criticism of the system feels like criticising yourself. The social mechanism is distributed. Stand up and say "excuse me, this is bullshit" and the community does the work of suppression without any coordination.

**Key distinction**: High pay and RSUs do not determine political beliefs one-to-one. They do help explain why "I hate this" often stops short of "I will risk my job, my career, and my vesting to stop this."

---

### V. THE CRABS IN THE BUCKET: What Happens When Someone Stands Up (~500 words)

**Purpose**: Document the pattern — worker resistance is real but structurally outmatched. Avoid flattening workers into villains while showing the actual conflict terrain.

**The pattern**:

- **The 2018 Google Walkout**: Over 20,000 employees walked out protesting Andy Rubin's $90M severance. Within a year, four of seven organisers had left. Google introduced policies restricting political speech and became less transparent — measures that could suppress activism.
- **Timnit Gebru** (December 2020): Co-lead of Google's Ethical AI team, fired after being ordered to retract a research paper on risks of large language models. Co-lead Margaret Mitchell fired months later. The Alphabet Workers Union called it _"an attack on the people who are trying to make Google's technology more ethical."_
- **Project Nimbus** (2024): Google fired 28 workers after protests over a $1.2B contract with the Israeli government. Firings rose to more than 50 according to organisers. Workers filed an NLRB complaint alleging unfair dismissal.
- **Microsoft** (2025): Fired employees who interrupted a company event to protest the Israel-Gaza war. Five years earlier, Microsoft had divested from an Israeli facial recognition startup partly in response to internal pressure. The window closed.

**The wider chill**: Since 2023, over half a million tech jobs cut. Companies rolling back DEI commitments wholesale — Meta cut DEI spending by 53%, Tesla by 84%. Google, Amazon, and Meta abandoned diversity hiring targets and dissolved DEI teams. The message was clear: **shut up**.

**The peer dynamic**: The Damore affair (2017) showed that Google's internal forums contained support for an anti-diversity memo, with employees offering private thanks to its author. The crabs pulled each other back into the bucket.

**The framing**: "This is not a story of no resistance. It is a story of resistance without enough structural power." The Alphabet Workers Union launched in 2021 with 400 members — but with 1,400 members out of 150,000 workers, it's a fraction of what's needed.

---

### VI. THE REVERSIBLE PROMISE: Ethics Statements Are Not Power (~300 words)

**Purpose**: Convert the case studies into a general lesson about the fragility of corporate ethics language.

**The evidence**:

- Google's 2018 AI Principles page explicitly listed "AI applications we will not pursue," including weapons and surveillance violating internationally accepted norms.
- The same page now carries an update note dated 4 February 2025 pointing readers elsewhere for the latest principles.
- The current (2025) Google AI Principles PDF emphasises proceeding where benefits "substantially outweigh" foreseeable risks. The old explicit "we will not pursue" list is gone.

**The point**: This shift does not prove any single contract is illegitimate on its own. It does support the broader claim that internal ethics frameworks are contingent and can be redefined as strategy changes. Ethics language can be sincere in the moment and still reversible under pressure.

**Key line**: "Policy language is a terrain of struggle, not a guarantee."

**The connection to the Hacker Ethic**: Andreessen Horowitz spent $89 million in the 2024 election cycle. Peter Thiel gave $1.25M to Trump's 2016 campaign. The PayPal founder who built his fortune on the early internet — cypherpunk-adjacent, libertarian in the old sense — ended up as an architect of the new right. When your wealth depends on a specific regulatory environment, a specific tax regime, a specific labour market, you develop politics to match.

---

### VII. THE RECKONING: What "We" Actually Means (~400 words)

**Purpose**: The angriest section — and it earns its anger.

**The specific betrayal**: The _knowledge workers_ — the people who _understand_ these systems, who can see exactly how the surveillance works, how the data is used, how the algorithmic manipulation functions — those people choosing to build the machine anyway. The factory worker in 1905 might not have understood capital's inner workings. The software engineer in 2024 has no such excuse.

**The social dynamic**: The false "we" works because it offers something the genuine counterculture could never offer: the relief of being on the winning side. For people who grew up as outsiders, as weirdos, as the ones who got beaten up for liking computers — the offer of belonging to the powerful group is intoxicating. Even if the belonging is a lie. _Especially_ if the belonging is a lie.

**The test**: Are you included in the decisions? Do your interests actually align with theirs when it matters? When the billionaires pushed for policies that benefited their capital, did it benefit you? When they fought regulation, did the outcome serve you?

**The answer, for the vast majority**: No. The "we" was always conditional, always extractive, always going to end at the point where your interests diverged from theirs. The hacker who thinks they're part of the revolutionary in-group is actually just labour. The moment they stop being useful, the "we" evaporates.

**Snowden's testimony**: _"People who make spreadsheets, people who make weather apps, people who just wanted to create family tree websites, all of them are being bought out, are being subverted, are being corrupted, are being incentivised into building capabilities into their platforms that fundamentally work against the public."_

---

### VIII. CLOSING: Build Better Institutions (~400 words)

**Purpose**: End with a programme, not just a diagnosis. Replace disappointment with strategy.

**The call**: This isn't a demand to go live in a commune. It's a demand for _clarity_. Know who you actually work for. Know whose interests your code serves. Apply the original hacker scepticism — the scepticism that used to be aimed at governments and corporations — to the specific government and corporation you're currently inside.

**What actually changes outcomes**:

- Worker organisation: unions, minority unions, solidarity structures — not individual heroism
- Contractor inclusion: not just FTE moralism
- Community-run technical spaces: hackspaces, co-ops, public labs
- Democratic accountability for public contracts and AI procurement
- Professional norms tied to refusal, backed collectively

**The counter-evidence**: The hackerspaces. Hackspace Manchester: member-run, no staff, elected directors, over 300 members. Edinburgh Hacklab. London Hackspace. The UK has over 60 active hackspaces. Still anarchosocialist by default. Still full of people grumbling about the state and teaching each other things for free. Nobody earns $164,000 a year for it. And yet they embody every principle of the Hacker Ethic more faithfully than any FAANG company ever did. If the hacker ethic only survives in spaces where people _aren't_ being paid six figures, that tells you everything about what the money did.

**The reframe**: Stop expecting subculture to save us. Start building structures that make solidarity durable.

**Closing image**: The question is whether the people who left those rooms to go work at the tech companies ever actually left, or whether they just got very good at pretending they did. The counterculture didn't die everywhere. It just got very expensive to maintain in a few specific postcodes.

## Tone & Voice Notes

- **First person plural** — "we" — because this is self-criticism as much as accusation. The author _was there_. That's the authority.
- **Angry but controlled** — fury is more effective than polemic noise. Sharp, unsentimental, not preachy.
- **Specific, not abstract** — name names, name places, name the mechanisms. Generality lets people off the hook.
- **British cadence, no hedging** — short sentences when landing a point. Long sentences for building the argument.
- **Profanity is permitted, not mandatory** — use it where the plainness of it reinforces the point. The closing "get fucked" from the original prompt earns its place if the argument has been built properly.
- **Distinguish evidence from inference** — label disputed facts as disputed (organiser vs company claims). Date-stamp claims. Use 1–2 vivid lines from personal experience per section, then cash them out with facts.
- **What to avoid**: "nerds are fascists now" overstatement; flattening all tech workers into executives; treating a few anecdotes as universal proof; ahistorical nostalgia for a pure hacker past; purity politics and "I am the only one who saw this."

## Counterarguments to Address (In Good Faith)

### "Most tech workers are socially liberal"

Agree on surface truth. Distinguish social liberalism from anti-capitalist or labour politics. Point back to workplace behaviour and contract politics, not self-description.

### "Defence/AI contracts are complicated; some uses save lives"

Concede complexity. The essay is not "all government work is evil." Focus on democratic accountability, transparency, labour voice, and who bears risks versus who captures gains.

### "People need jobs; not everyone can martyr themselves"

Agree completely. Use this to _strengthen_ the case for unions and collective action: individual heroism is not a strategy. That's the whole point of Section VIII.

### "You're romanticising hackerspaces"

Acknowledge flaws, exclusions, and conflicts in any community space. Restate the narrower claim: governance forms matter; member-run institutions create different incentives than shareholder firms.

## What This Essay Is NOT

- Not an AI essay — that's a separate piece
- Not a general critique of capitalism — it's specifically about the betrayal of a specific subculture's specific stated values, and the category error of treating subculture as politics
- Not nostalgia — the early hacker culture had its own problems; the point is what happened to its anti-authoritarian _content_ and why subcultural identity alone was never enough
- Not a flattening of all tech workers into villains — the essay must acknowledge resistance and explain why it was structurally outmatched
