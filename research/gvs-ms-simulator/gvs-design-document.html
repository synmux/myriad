1. System Overview

This project implements a Galvanic Vestibular Stimulation (GVS) device based on the ESP32 microcontroller. The system delivers a precisely controlled, bidirectional DC current (±3 mA maximum) through electrodes placed on the mastoid processes behind the ears. By modulating the current direction and intensity, the device can induce perceived shifts in balance — creating the sensation of lateral movement, G-forces, or tilting.

The primary use case is integration with games and simulators. The ESP32 receives telemetry data (e.g., lateral G-force values from a flight simulator or racing game) over USB serial or Wi-Fi, and translates these into corresponding current waveforms that the user feels as balance shifts in real time.

2. GVS Theory of Operation

Galvanic Vestibular Stimulation works by passing a small DC current through the vestibular system via surface electrodes on the mastoid processes (the bony prominences behind each ear). The current modulates the firing rate of vestibular afferent neurons:

  • Cathode side (negative): Increases the firing rate of vestibular neurons — the brain perceives acceleration towards the cathode.
  • Anode side (positive): Decreases the firing rate — the brain perceives acceleration away from the anode.

With bilateral electrode placement, passing current from left to right causes the user to sway or feel a pull to the right (towards the cathode), and vice versa. The effect is proportional to current magnitude within the safe range of approximately 0.5–3 mA. Research literature typically uses stimulation currents up to 4 mA, though most subjects find 2–3 mA produces strong effects.

Key insight from the reference project: The creator's ESP32 DAC outputs 0–3.3V. At 3.3V, +3 mA flows in one direction; at 0V, −3 mA flows in the other. The midpoint (1.65V) corresponds to zero current. This is elegantly achieved using an op-amp configured as a voltage-controlled current source with a 1.65V reference.

Data Flow

The PC game or simulator sends lateral G-force telemetry to the ESP32 over USB serial (or Wi-Fi UDP for wireless operation). The ESP32's firmware maps the G-force value to a DAC output voltage between 0V and 3.3V (where 1.65V = zero current). The analogue voltage drives a voltage-controlled current source (VCCS) built around an op-amp, which outputs a proportional bipolar current through the electrodes.

4. Circuit Design

The core of this design is a voltage-controlled current source (VCCS) based on an op-amp. This topology ensures the output current remains proportional to the input voltage regardless of varying head impedance (which can change with skin moisture, electrode contact, and individual physiology).

4.1 — The Voltage-Controlled Current Source

The design uses a modified inverting amplifier configuration. One op-amp input receives the DAC voltage from the ESP32; the other input is connected to a 1.65V reference rail (midpoint of the 3.3V supply) via a sense resistor. The op-amp adjusts its output to maintain both inputs at the same voltage, forcing a proportional current through the sense resistor and hence through the load (the user's head).

    ─── Operating Principle ─────────────────────────────────────────────────

    The op-amp forces V(+) = V(−)

    • V(+) = V_DAC  (from ESP32, 0 to 3.3V)
    • V(−) = 1.65V + (I_out × R_s)

    Therefore:  I_out = (V_DAC − 1.65V) / R_s

    With R_s = 560Ω (closest E24 value to 550Ω):
    • V_DAC = 3.3V  →  I = (3.3 − 1.65) / 560  ≈  +2.95 mA
    • V_DAC = 1.65V →  I = (1.65 − 1.65) / 560  =   0.00 mA
    • V_DAC = 0.0V  →  I = (0.0 − 1.65) / 560   ≈  −2.95 mA

The 1.65V reference is created using a simple resistor divider from the 3.3V supply, buffered by the second channel of the dual MCP6002 op-amp. This ensures a stable, low-impedance midpoint reference.

4.3 — Compliance Voltage & Supply Options

⚠ Critical Design Note — Compliance Voltage

The op-amp must swing its output high enough to push the desired current through all series resistances: R_sense (560Ω) + R_safety (1kΩ) + R_head (typically 1–5 kΩ with TENS pads). For ±3 mA with a 2 kΩ head impedance, the op-amp output needs to reach ≈12.3V — far beyond a 3.3V supply rail.

The reference project's "24 mA worst-case at 1kΩ" implies a ~24V op-amp supply, confirming the original design uses voltage boosting. Below, two prototype approaches are presented.

Option A — 3.3V Supply (Simple Prototype, Reduced Current)

If you want the simplest possible first build, power the op-amp from the ESP32's 3.3V rail and reduce R_safety to 220Ω. The achievable current will be lower, but the circuit is inherently very safe:

  • With R_head = 500Ω (good electrode contact + gel): I_max ≈ 1.65 / (560 + 220 + 500) ≈ 1.3 mA
  • With R_head = 2 kΩ (typical dry contact): I_max ≈ 1.65 / (560 + 220 + 2000) ≈ 0.6 mA
  • Absolute worst case (electrodes shorted): 3.3V / (560 + 220) ≈ 4.2 mA — within safe range

This is enough to perceive the GVS effect (literature reports thresholds around 0.5 mA), but the sensation will be subtle. Use conductive electrode gel to minimise skin impedance and maximise the available current. Use the MCP6002 for both the VCCS and reference buffer.

Option B — Boosted Supply (Full Performance, Recommended)

For the full ±3 mA capability matching the reference project, add a boost converter module to step up the 5V USB input to 24V. This powers the VCCS op-amp, giving it enough output swing to drive current through the full load impedance.

  • Boost converter: XL6009 or MT3608 module (5V → 24V, adjustable output via trimpot). These cost ~£2 and require no external components.
  • VCCS op-amp: Replace MCP6002 with LM358N (DIP-8, operates up to 32V, ~£0.30). Not rail-to-rail, but with 24V supply and 1.65V reference, there is ample headroom.
  • Reference buffer: Keep the MCP6002 on the ESP32's 3.3V rail to buffer the 1.65V reference. The MCP6002's output feeds the sense resistor just as before.
  • Safety resistor: Keep R_safety at 1kΩ. Worst case: 24V / 1kΩ = 24 mA — matching the original design.

With 24V supply, R_safety = 1kΩ, and R_head = 2 kΩ:

V_out = 1.65 + 0.003 × (560 + 1000 + 2000) = 12.3V — well within the LM358's output range on a 24V rail.

Why two op-amps? The LM358 runs on the 24V boost rail (for output swing), while the MCP6002 runs on 3.3V (for accurate 1.65V reference buffering). The ESP32 DAC input (0–3.3V) connects directly to the LM358's non-inverting input — this is safe because the LM358 accepts any input voltage within its supply range, and 0–3.3V is well within 0–24V. The 1.65V reference from the MCP6002 connects through R_sense to the LM358's inverting input.

4.4 — Pull-Down Resistor

A 10kΩ pull-down resistor between the DAC output and the 1.65V reference ensures that if the ESP32 loses power, resets, or the DAC pin floats, the voltage defaults to 1.65V (zero current) rather than an indeterminate value.

Key Firmware Features

  • Slew rate limiting: Gradual ramp between current levels to prevent jarring sensations and reduce skin irritation. Target transition time of ~50 ms between extremes.
  • Watchdog timeout: If no valid telemetry data is received for 500 ms, the output ramps to midpoint (zero current). This prevents sustained stimulation if the game crashes or disconnects.
  • Configurable parameters: Maximum current limit, dead zone around zero (to avoid tingling at near-zero currents), slew rate, and input mapping curve — all adjustable via serial commands.
  • ADC feedback (optional): GPIO34 can monitor the voltage across the electrode return path to verify actual current delivery and detect electrode disconnection.

Serial Protocol (Simple)

The PC sends a single float value per line representing normalised lateral G-force:

  • -1.0 = maximum left G-force (full current right-to-left)
  • 0.0 = no lateral force (zero current)
  • +1.0 = maximum right G-force (full current left-to-right)

6. Safety Design

⚠ Defence in Depth

Multiple independent safety layers ensure no single failure can deliver dangerous current.

Layer Mechanism Failure Mode Protected
1. Hardware current limit 1kΩ series resistor on output Op-amp latch-up, software failure, DAC malfunction
2. Supply voltage limit Option A: Op-amp on 3.3V (output capped at rail). Option B: 1kΩ safety resistor limits worst-case to 24 mA. Op-amp latch-up or runaway output. Option A inherently limits voltage; Option B relies on R_safety.
3. Pull-down to midpoint 10kΩ from DAC to 1.65V rail ESP32 reset, power loss, floating DAC pin
4. Firmware watchdog Ramp to zero if no data for 500 ms Game crash, USB disconnect, Wi-Fi dropout
5. Firmware bounds check DAC clamped to configurable max range Erroneous telemetry values, buffer overflow
6. Slew rate limiting Max transition rate capped in firmware Sudden current spikes, nausea reduction
7. Physical kill switch Momentary button to force DAC to midpoint User override for any uncomfortable situation

7. Electrodes & Placement

Self-adhesive TENS/EMS electrode pads are the simplest and safest option for a prototype. They provide consistent skin contact, are designed for electrical stimulation, and are widely available. Use small electrodes (~2×2 cm or circular ~3 cm²) for better current density and stronger vestibular effect. Research suggests smaller electrode areas produce more focused stimulation.

Connection

Use standard 2mm pin or 3.5mm snap connectors (matching the electrode pads) with lead wires running to the circuit board. Keep leads reasonably short to minimise noise pickup. For a wearable version, the entire circuit could be mounted on a headband or behind-the-ear housing.
